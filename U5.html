    Listening and Speaking

    Text Analysis

    This lesson's content is taken from the “Fourteenth Five-Year Plan” vocational education national planning textbook published by the Higher Education Press, from Unit 5 of "English 2 Basic Module." The focus is on history and culture, and the section includes a Warming Up and Listening and Speaking activities centered around art. The lesson features listening activities involving extended dialogues about artists and their works, leading to spoken exercises discussing exhibits. Students are expected to understand information about ancient civilizations and to be able to ask for and provide information about various artists and artworks.

    Student Analysis

    The students in this class are first-year vocational school students with some basic English skills, but there is a significant divergence in their abilities: some students are very interested and can communicate fluently in English, while others have low interest, are reluctant to speak, and feel intimidated. From previous learning, they have some understanding of ancient civilizations and a preliminary knowledge of the most representative ancient civilizations of different countries. On the topic of ancient civilizations, students can name some countries and generally identify categories of works, but they lack the language skills and knowledge to describe artists and artworks in English. Therefore, teachers should design educational activities that progress from easy to difficult, from simple to complex, from mechanical imitation to substitution exercises, and then to practical language use, gradually laying out steps to minimize potential difficulties and frustrations for students.

    Instructional Objectives

    1. Students should be able to understand dialogues about ancient civilizations and extract key information.
    2. Students should be able to exchange information about famous domestic and international artists and their renowned artworks.
    3. Students should be able to appreciate the greatness of ancient civilizations and their profound impact on modern civilization during conversational exchanges.

    Key and Difficult Points

    Teaching Focus: Students should be able to discuss famous domestic and international artists and their renowned artworks.
    Teaching Challenge: Students should be able to proficiently use vocabulary and sentence structures to describe artists and artworks, such as:
    artist, works, impress, originator, sculptor, influence, portrait, curious, vitality, attractive, enlighten, indeed
    What artist or works impress you most in the exhibition?
    I think I am most attracted to…
    He is thought to be one of the greatest artists and sculptors of the 20th century.
    I think even today we are influenced by his ideas.
    Which work is your favorite?

    Reading and Writing

    Text Analysis

    This lesson's content is drawn from Unit 5's Reading and Writing section of the textbook, focusing on an article about the Silk Road. The text is structured in a "general-specific-general" format, beginning with an introduction to the concept of the "Silk Road," then detailing the origins, routes, and key figures of the maritime Silk Road, followed by the land routes and their historical roles, and concluding with a summary of the Silk Road's historical value. The article provides a thorough introduction to the history of the Silk Road and its contributions to human civilization from various perspectives. The text features distinctive stylistic traits and vivid language, integrating educational elements with political thought. By reading the article, students become familiar with and master the stylistic and linguistic features of humanities exposition; they use reading strategies like keyword analysis and employ explanation techniques such as examples, deduction, and induction to develop skills in summarizing the main theme and the central ideas of each paragraph. Through observation, analysis, and comparison, students recognize the expressive traits of humanities expository texts and, by combining the Silk Road's routes, origins, trade goods, and associated figures, gain an appreciation for ancient China's splendid history and its significant contributions to world civilization, strengthening their cultural confidence and actively promoting and spreading Chinese civilization.

    Student Analysis

    After the previous two lessons, students have become familiar with the topic of ancient civilizations, able to exchange information about famous domestic and international artists and their works, and from these discussions, appreciate the greatness of ancient civilizations and their profound impacts on modern civilization. The previous lesson's homework on "Introducing Fine Cultural Relics of the Silk Road" also gave students a deeper background understanding of the Silk Road topic. While students have grasped some sentences about ancient civilizations and have a basic understanding of the structure of expository texts, they still lack the language needed to write expositions about ancient civilizations and need a deeper understanding of the Silk Road's role and greatness through the study and analysis of historical facts.

    Instructional Objectives

    1. Students should be able to understand articles about the Silk Road, use keywords to grasp the text's content, and employ various explanatory methods like examples, deduction, and induction to summarize the article's theme and identify details within each paragraph.
    2. Through observation, analysis, and comparison, students should become familiar with and master the structure and linguistic features of humanities expository texts, express their views on the topic of the Silk Road, and write short introductory essays about the topic.
    By considering the Silk Road's routes, origins, traded goods, and associated figures, students should appreciate the glorious history of ancient China and its outstanding contributions to world civilization, strengthen their national cultural confidence, and actively promote and spread Chinese civilization.

    Key and Difficult Points

    Teaching Focus: Students should be able to understand articles about the Silk Road and, through observation, analysis, and comparison, become familiar with and master the structure and linguistic features of humanities expository texts, express their views on the Silk Road, and write short essays on the topic.
    Teaching Challenge: In the process of writing essays on the Silk Road, students should be able to correctly use related structures and language, such as: link, actual, route, maritime, peak, emissary, boost, western, exchange, as early as, search for, China fever.
    Examples of challenging content include:
    Did you know that the Silk Road was neither an actual road nor a single route?
    During the Eastern Han Dynasty, the Chinese emissary Gan Ying was sent to search for "Da Qin," the Chinese name for the Roman Empire at that time.

    Language Practice & Vocabulary

    Text Analysis

    This lesson's content is selected from Unit 5's Language Practice section of the textbook, divided into Grammar and Vocabulary parts. The material revolves around the theme of ancient civilizations, focusing on the basic usage of object clauses and key vocabulary related to ancient civilizations. Object clauses are a major grammatical topic. Students will summarize the basic usage of object clauses through activities such as observing example sentences, identifying markings, completing sentences, and engaging in dialogues. This will help them perceive differences in thinking between Western and Eastern cultures and apply this understanding to enhance their practical language skills. By using phrases and words in specific contexts, students consolidate the language points of this unit and achieve real-life application.

    Student Analysis

    After middle school, students have some understanding of object clauses, but their grasp of related knowledge is not solid, and their understanding is not deep enough. They still need systematic and effective learning and practice to master the basic usage of object clauses. Through the first four lessons of this unit, students have accumulated some vocabulary related to ancient civilizations but still need to continually apply this knowledge to solidify their language skills.

    Instructional Objectives

    1. Students should master the basic usage of object clauses and be able to use them accurately.
    2. Students should be able to accurately use key vocabulary related to ancient civilizations in specific contexts, such as: boost, link, actual, treasure, exchange, as early as, date back to, attract to, search for, etc.
    3. Through the study and practical application of grammatical knowledge, students experience English learning methods and develop good study habits.
    By studying object clauses, students understand the differences in thought expressed in language between Western and Eastern cultures.

    Key and Difficult Points

    Teaching Focus:
    Master the basic form and usage of object clauses and use them correctly to describe content related to ancient civilizations.
    Accurately use key vocabulary related to ancient civilizations in specific contexts.
    Teaching Challenges:
    Correct use of introductory words for object clauses.
    Correct analysis of sentence structures containing object clauses and understanding their meanings.

    Culture Understanding &Group Work

    Text Analysis

    This lesson integrates the "Culture Understanding" and "Group Work" sections from Unit 5 of the textbook. The "Culture Understanding" section is an expository text that introduces Marco Polo and his travelogues. The article has a clear structure: it begins with an overall introduction to Marco Polo and his "Travels of Marco Polo," then details his experiences in China, and finally discusses the positive impact of "The Travels of Marco Polo" on enhancing understanding and communication between the East and the West. By reading the text, students expand their knowledge, learn the stories behind "The Travels of Marco Polo," appreciate the legendary tales of ancient East-West exchanges, and recognize Marco Polo's contributions to improving East-West interactions. The "Group Work" section creates a real task to test students' learning outcomes. Students deepen their understanding of ancient Chinese civilization and cultural treasures through tasks such as learning basic information about the Mogao Caves, searching online for relevant information, creating slideshows about the Mogao Caves, and presenting them, all while developing teamwork and innovative skills.

    Student Analysis

    Through previous learning, students have become familiar with and mastered the stylistic features and language characteristics of humanities expository texts, accumulating a significant number of phrases describing ancient civilizations and becoming more proficient in using object clauses. Additionally, students have developed some capability in collecting, organizing, and synthesizing information, and have gained a deeper appreciation and understanding of the profound impacts of ancient Eastern and Western civilizations on global culture. Students generally enjoy completing learning tasks in groups and have some teamwork skills. However, their ability to analyze, prepare for, and complete tasks still needs to be further enhanced through continuous practice.

    Instructional Objectives

    1. Students should be able to accurately grasp the main idea of the article, express personal opinions on its content, and appreciate Marco Polo's contributions to enhancing East-West exchanges and understanding.
    2. Students should be able to collect and integrate information based on prompts, using the correct structure and language to introduce the Mogao Caves, thereby deepening their understanding of ancient Chinese civilization and cultural treasures while cultivating teamwork and innovative skills.
    3. Students should be able to perform self-evaluations, summarize and reflect on learning methods and strategies, develop good learning habits, and enhance their language learning and application skills.

    Key and Difficult Points

    Teaching Focus: Students should be able to collect and synthesize information, use the correct structure and language to introduce the Mogao Caves, and complete learning tasks.
    Teaching Challenge: When introducing the Mogao Caves, students should be able to appropriately use the structure and language typical of humanities expository texts and correctly use object clauses.
